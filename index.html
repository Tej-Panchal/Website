The Story

Switchbacks and Stack Traces

I spend my weekends reading trails. I spent three years reading systems. Supply chains, it turns out, ask the same question as both.

On a hike, a wrong turn doesn't announce itself right away — you feel it a mile later, when the trail stops matching the map, and by then you're already off course. Debugging production software taught me the exact same lesson from the other direction: the crash a customer reports is almost never where the problem started. It's the visible symptom of a decision made several steps earlier that nobody flagged as risky at the time — a delayed API response, a sync job that ran late, a queue nobody was watching until it backed up.

Three years as a software engineer trained me to stop reacting to the symptom and start tracing backward to the actual fork in the road. Supply chains have the same shape. A stockout on a shelf is rarely a shelf problem — it's a forecast, a lead time, or a handoff that broke several steps upstream. That's the instinct I'm bringing from engineering into supply chain: read the whole path, not just the point where it finally became visible.

I'm currently pursuing a Master of Science in Supply Chain Management at the University of Washington's Foster School of Business, building on almost three years as a software engineer automating QA pipelines, building the monitoring dashboards that flagged problems before customers did, and working cross-functionally with operations teams whenever something needed fixing at the root.

That combination — engineering rigor plus a habit of tracing failures back to their source — is what I bring to supply chain, program management, and operations roles. Outside of work, you'll usually find me actually testing that instinct on a trail somewhere in the Pacific Northwest, or on a badminton court.
